About the Team

The Nasa Technomos is a highly motivated and hardworking team of 14 individuals. We are passionate about robotics and are committed to achieving success through collaboration and innovation. We also have a sister team, the Robomos, who work alongside us to push our limits.

Together, we foster an environment of growth, teamwork, and perseverance, which fuels our passion for robotics!

Our Coaches

We have 7 dedicated coaches with diverse experiences in engineering, programming, and robotics. Their guidance helps us learn and grow. However, it’s the students who do the bulk of the work, coming up with creative solutions to challenges and building our robots.

Our coaches provide invaluable mentorship and insights, but it’s the students’ hard work and dedication that make the real difference.

What is FRC?

The FIRST Robotics Competition (FRC) is an exciting and challenging event where high school students collaborate to design, build, and program robots. Each year, a new game is revealed at the FRC kickoff, and teams like ours have six weeks to create a robot that can compete in various challenges.

FRC is not just about robots—it's about learning, teamwork, and making lifelong memories!

FRC Robotics Glossary

Accelerometer

A sensor that measures the acceleration force in multiple axes, often used to detect robot movement or tilt.

Actuator

A device that converts energy into motion, often used to move parts of the robot, such as arms, wheels, or doors.

Autonomous Mode

The period at the beginning of the match when the robot operates without human control, often using pre-programmed instructions.

Ballast

Additional weight or material added to a robot to improve stability, balance, or meet weight requirements.

Ball Shooter

A mechanism designed to shoot balls or other objects into a goal or target.

Bench Testing

Testing robot components outside the robot, often in a controlled environment, to check for performance and reliability.

Brake

A mechanical or electrical system used to stop or slow down the robot or specific parts of the robot.

CAD (Computer-Aided Design)

A software tool used to create detailed 2D or 3D designs of robot parts and assemblies before building them physically.

Chassis

The main structural frame of the robot that holds all the components together, including the wheels, motors, and drivetrain.

Climber

A robot mechanism designed to scale a structure or goal during a match, often required to complete certain challenges.

Collider

Any physical contact between robots or parts of a robot during a match, often leading to damage or penalties.

Code

The programming instructions that control the behavior of a robot. Code determines how the robot responds to sensors and operator inputs.

Control System

The set of electronics and software that allows the robot to operate. It includes motors, sensors, and controllers that interact with the robot's code.

Defensive Robot

A robot built to block or impede opponents from scoring or performing tasks, often relying on physical barriers or obstacles.

Driver Station

A computer system used by drivers to control the robot during matches. It includes joysticks, buttons, and a display showing robot diagnostics.

Drivetrain

The system of motors, wheels, and gears that allows a robot to move across the field. This includes wheels, tracks, and other mobility systems.

Drop Zone

A specific area on the field where game pieces are placed by the human players during the match. Robots can retrieve these pieces to score points.

Encoder

A sensor that measures the rotation of a motor or wheel, allowing the robot to track its position and speed more accurately.

Endgame

The final phase of a match, often involving special actions or tasks, such as climbing or scoring bonus points. The endgame can be a critical part of determining the match winner.

Gripper

A mechanism used to pick up and hold onto game pieces, such as balls, cubes, or other objects on the field.

Gyroscope

A sensor used to measure the robot's orientation or angular velocity, helping the robot maintain balance and control during movement.

Infeed

The process of transferring game pieces into the robot's mechanism to be manipulated, moved, or scored.

Intake

A robot mechanism used to collect or "pick up" game pieces from the field, such as balls or other objects, often using rollers or belts.

Joystick

A control device used by the driver to control the robot’s movement and actions. Joysticks are often paired with buttons or switches.

Kickoff

The official start of the FRC season, where the game rules and challenges are revealed to all teams at the same time.

LED

Light Emitting Diode, a type of indicator light used on the robot to display the robot's status, such as active, disabled, or specific modes.

Lift

A mechanism designed to raise or elevate other parts of the robot or game pieces, often to reach higher goals or platforms.

Limit Switch

A type of sensor that detects physical contact or position of a robot part. It is often used to stop movement at a specific point to prevent damage.

Match

A timed competition between two or more teams, where robots attempt to score points by completing tasks or challenges outlined in the game rules.

Motor Controller

A device that regulates the speed and direction of a motor by receiving signals from the robot's control system and translating them into motor commands.

Outreach

Activities and events that promote robotics, science, and technology in the community, often used to inspire students to pursue STEM careers.

Pit

The area where teams work on their robots before and after matches. The pit is where robots are inspected, repaired, and prepared for competition.

Power Distribution Panel (PDP)

The electrical component that distributes power from the battery to the motors and other parts of the robot.

Robot Controller

The computer that runs the robot’s software and processes sensor data to control the robot's movement and actions.

Robot Design

The process of planning, building, and refining the robot to perform well in the competition. It involves both mechanical and electrical design as well as programming.

Scoring

The process of achieving points in the game by completing tasks such as shooting balls, placing objects, or achieving specific robot actions.

Scout

A team member responsible for gathering information on other teams' robots, performance, and strategies during the competition.

Servo Motor

A small motor with built-in feedback control, often used for precise control of smaller mechanisms, such as arms or claws.

Shifter

A mechanism that changes the robot's gear ratio, typically between high and low gears, to adjust speed or torque as needed.

Subassembly

A smaller part or unit that forms a part of the larger robot. Examples include arms, wheels, or sensors, which can be tested separately before integration.

Telescoping Arm

A type of arm that extends and retracts, allowing the robot to reach taller structures or grasp higher game pieces.

Teleoperated Mode (Teleop)

The second part of the match where human drivers control the robot via joysticks, cameras, and sensors to complete tasks and score points.

Torque

The measure of rotational force applied by the motors, often related to how fast or powerful the robot moves, especially in climbing or heavy lifting tasks.

Wheel Encoder

A sensor that measures the rotation of a wheel, helping to calculate the robot's speed and position on the field.

Workstation

The area in the pit where team members prepare and monitor robot code, manage tools, or perform testing.

Wheelbase

The distance between the front and rear wheels of the robot, affecting how the robot turns and drives.

Zeroing

The process of setting a sensor or motor’s position to a specific reference point, often used in programming to ensure accuracy.
